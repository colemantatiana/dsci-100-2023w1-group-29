Cleveland Heart Disease Binary Classification

One of the leading causes of death within Canada and globally is heart disease. Heart disease comprises a range of multifaceted conditions including heart failure and artery blockage. These conditions can be influenced by a myriad of factors such as genetics and lifestyle. In this project, we will be using binary classification (present or absent) to answer the question: does a patient have heart disease based on the analysis of three key attributes- maximum heart rate achieved, age, and ST-wave depression induced by exercise? By investigating the relationship between these variables and the presence of heart disease, we aim to create a predictive model that can effectively identify individuals at risk of heart disease. This classification will be conducted on the Cleveland heart disease dataset which contains 303 entries across 14 different variables.

names <- colnames(heart_train |> select(age, trestbps, chol, thalach, oldpeak))

accuracies <- tibble(size = integer(), 
                     model_string = character(), 
                     accuracy = numeric())

knn_spec <- nearest_neighbor(weight_func = "rectangular", 
                             neighbors = tune()) |>
     set_engine("kknn") |>
     set_mode("classification")

heart_vfold <- vfold_cv(heart_train, v = 5, strata = diseased)

n_total <- length(names)
selected <- c()
for (i in 1:n_total) {
    accs <- list()
    models <- list()
    for (j in 1:length(names)) {
        preds_new <- c(selected, names[[j]])
        model_string <- paste("diseased", "~", paste(preds_new, collapse="+"))
        heart_recipe <- recipe(as.formula(model_string), 
                                data = heart_train) |>
                          step_scale(all_predictors()) |>
                          step_center(all_predictors())
        acc <- workflow() |>
          add_recipe(heart_recipe) |>
          add_model(knn_spec) |>
          tune_grid(resamples = heart_vfold, grid = 10) |>
          collect_metrics() |>
          filter(.metric == "accuracy") |>
          summarize(mx = max(mean))
        acc <- acc$mx |> unlist()
        accs[[j]] <- acc
        models[[j]] <- model_string
    }
    jstar <- which.max(unlist(accs))
    accuracies <- accuracies |> 
      add_row(size = i, 
              model_string = models[[jstar]], 
              accuracy = accs[[jstar]])
    selected <- c(selected, names[[jstar]])
    names <- names[-jstar]
}

Methods

From the data set, we have identified three relevant predictors for our analysis: (1) thalach, maximum heart rate achieved, (2) age, (3) and oldpeak, ST-wave depression induced by exercise. Higher maximum heart rates could possibly indicate better cardiovascular fitness. Age is a well-studied risk factor for heart disease; showing that the risk of heart disease occurrence increases with age. Lastly, the degree of ST-wave depression induced by exercise relative to rest is associated with a greater likelihood of heart disease as it reaches higher levels.

For our initial visualization, we have shown two of our predictor variables as a function of the third, age. We will use scatter plots as a primary technique to visualize our results. Within the plots, we plan to use both colour and shape to distinguish between individuals with or without heart disease. Additionally, we would use background colors to indicate the diagnosis of our model. We hope to find any linear or non-linear separations between clusters of data points, as well as any outliers or data points that do not conform to general trends/patterns. This will be crucial in understanding and constructing the classification model.

Expected Results and Significance

We expect to develop a classification model that accurately predicts the presence of heart disease based on the selected variables. Furthermore, we hope to visually identify patterns or trends between these predictor variables and the presence of heart disease. The significance of these findings may contribute to the early detection of individuals at risk and perhaps reduce the severity and progression of heart disease. As well, more personalized care can be administered to patients in different risk groups categorized by these models. In terms of impact, these classification models can lead to better patient outcomes and better public health. Personalized care has the potential to optimize resource allocation, which would create a more efficient healthcare system. Some additional questions can be raised about the potential interaction between the selected variables and other factors. Do the selected variables interact with other risk factors such as genetics and lifestyle to influence the risk of heart disease? Are there any differences in predictive models for heart disease when data is separated by sex? A more detailed analysis could be performed to develop more advanced predictive models that can integrate these proposed wider range of variables. This could in turn be used to evaluated the efficacy of preventive measures for heart diseases.
